Scaling IoT Telemetry Ingestion: Protocol Heterogeneity

In progress — March 2026

At 10,000+ devices, the ingestion problem is not throughput — it is heterogeneity. A real-world IoT deployment does not speak one protocol. Field devices are chosen for power constraints, range, and cost, not for the convenience of the backend. The platform has to handle what the hardware sends.

The protocols and their characteristics

MQTT

TCP-based, persistent connections, three QoS levels (0 = fire and forget, 1 = at least once, 2 = exactly once). Most suitable for always-connected devices with reliable network access. The broker (e.g. Mosquitto, EMQX) manages subscriptions.

LoRaWAN

Low-power wide-area network. Devices send infrequently (duty cycle restrictions) over long range. The network server handles device registration and message routing — your ingestion service subscribes to the NS. Payloads are typically small and binary-encoded.

BLE

Short range, connectionless advertising or GATT connections. Typically requires a gateway device that bridges BLE to IP. The ingestion service receives data from the gateway, not directly from the sensor. Gateway reliability becomes a variable.

Why a generic ingestion layer fails

The tempting design is a single ingest service with pluggable protocol adapters. This minimises code duplication and appears clean in a diagram. In practice, it creates a single point of failure — if the generic service has a bug triggered by LoRaWAN payload parsing, it can affect MQTT ingestion. Protocol-specific failure modes bleed into unrelated paths.

In the Jarvis platform, each protocol had its own ingest service. The MQTT service connected to the MQTT broker; the LoRaWAN service subscribed to the network server; the BLE service consumed from the gateway adapters. Each service normalised its protocol's payload to a common internal schema before handing off downstream. When the LoRaWAN network server had connectivity issues, MQTT ingestion was entirely unaffected.

Load characteristics and scaling

IoT device load is not uniform. Devices boot in batches, report on synchronized schedules, and produce burst traffic during business hours. Stateless ingest services on serverless compute (OCI Functions in this case) handle burst well — instances scale horizontally with load and idle away when traffic subsides. The cost model also aligns: you pay for actual device telemetry, not for idle pre-provisioned capacity.

Stateful services — the MQTT broker, the database — cannot scale this way and were managed separately on Kubernetes. Separating the stateless ingest layer from the stateful persistence layer is the key architectural boundary.
