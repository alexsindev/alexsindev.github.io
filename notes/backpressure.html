Backpressure in Event-Driven Systems

In progress — March 2026

Backpressure is what happens when a consumer cannot keep up with a producer. Without it, the producer keeps producing, the queue grows unbounded, memory fills, and the system falls over. With it, the producer slows down or the excess is handled in a defined way. Every event-driven system needs a backpressure story. Most systems discover they do not have one in production.

RabbitMQ: consumer prefetch

RabbitMQ's backpressure mechanism is basic.qos prefetch — you tell the broker how many unacknowledged messages a consumer may hold at once. If the consumer is processing slowly, the broker stops delivering new messages until acknowledgements arrive. The queue absorbs the burst; the consumer works at its own pace.

Setting prefetch too high removes the backpressure (the consumer is overwhelmed before it can ack). Setting it too low reduces throughput (the consumer idles waiting for delivery). The right value depends on message processing time and the number of consumer threads. In practice: start at 10–50 and measure queue depth under load.

Kafka: consumer group lag

Kafka does not push to consumers — consumers pull. A consumer reads at its own pace and commits offsets when ready. If processing is slow, the consumer falls behind; the topic partition accumulates lag. Kafka retains messages regardless, so no data is lost — the consumer catches up when capacity allows.

Consumer lag is the primary operational metric for Kafka pipelines. A rising lag number means the consumer cannot keep up. The response is to add consumer instances (up to the partition count) or to optimise the processing path. Kafka's backpressure is pull-based and observable; the trade-off is that lag accumulation can mean consumers are processing stale events.

Elixir GenStage: demand-driven pipelines

GenStage implements backpressure as a first-class protocol. Consumers declare demand — how many events they want — and producers only emit up to that demand. The demand signal flows upstream through the pipeline. A slow consumer reduces demand; the upstream producer slows or buffers accordingly. 

defmodule MyConsumer do
  use GenStage

  def init(_) do
    # Subscribe to producer, request max 10 events at a time
    {:consumer, :ok, subscribe_to: [{MyProducer, max_demand: 10}]}
  end

  def handle_events(events, _from, state) do
    Enum.each(events, &process/1)
    # Demand is automatically replenished after handle_events returns
    {:noreply, [], state}
  end
end

GenStage is the right choice when the processing pipeline is entirely within an Elixir application and you want backpressure without an external broker. Broadway (built on GenStage) adds producer integrations for RabbitMQ and Kafka, combining the demand-driven model with external message sources.

Choosing a strategy

If you are using RabbitMQ or Kafka as your message bus, use their native mechanisms first — prefetch and consumer group lag are well-understood and operationally visible. Reach for GenStage when you need fine-grained backpressure within an Elixir processing pipeline, particularly for CPU-bound work where you want to avoid overwhelming the scheduler.
