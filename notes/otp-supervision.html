The OTP Supervision Tree as an Architecture Pattern

In progress — March 2026

In most systems, the failure model is implicit. You find out what fails together when something goes wrong in production. In Elixir's OTP, the failure model is declared upfront in the supervision tree — and that declaration is the architecture. The tree tells you exactly what restarts when something crashes, and what it takes down with it.

The process boundary is the unit of isolation

In OTP, every meaningful piece of state lives in a process. Processes are isolated — a crash in one process cannot corrupt the memory of another. Supervisors watch processes and restart them according to a defined strategy: one_for_one (restart only the crashed process), one_for_all (restart everything under the supervisor), or rest_for_one (restart the crashed process and everything started after it). Choosing the right strategy for each supervisor is the act of declaring your failure model.

Scoping supervisors to logical domains

In the Singapore PIDS system, the supervisor tree was scoped to bus stop clusters. Each cluster ran under its own supervisor. A corrupted data event at one cluster — a malformed arrival prediction, a Redis timeout — triggered a restart of that cluster's processes, not the entire display network. The other 199 bus stops continued operating normally because they were under separate supervisors.

The alternative — a single supervisor for all display processes — would have made the blast radius of any failure equal to the entire system. Scoping supervisors correctly is the most important architectural decision in an OTP system.

The supervision tree as documentation

A well-structured supervision tree is the most honest piece of architecture documentation a system has. It shows exactly what processes exist, how they are related, and what happens when each one fails. In Cadmus's exam platform, each active exam session is a supervised process. The tree makes it immediately clear that exam sessions are isolated from each other — a crash in one exam cannot affect another. That is not a comment in the code; it is enforced by the runtime. 

# A supervisor scoped to a logical domain
defmodule PIDS.BusStop.Supervisor do
  use Supervisor

  def start_link(stop_id) do
    Supervisor.start_link(__MODULE__, stop_id, name: via(stop_id))
  end

  def init(stop_id) do
    children = [
      {PIDS.BusStop.DataFetcher, stop_id},
      {PIDS.BusStop.DisplayBroadcaster, stop_id}
    ]
    # one_for_one: a fetcher crash does not restart the broadcaster
    Supervisor.init(children, strategy: :one_for_one)
  end
end
